About the Region

The Heart of Northeastern Mindanao

Caraga (Region XIII) occupies the northeastern portion of Mindanao — bounded by the Bohol Sea to the north, the Philippine Sea to the east, Davao Region to the south, and Northern Mindanao to the west. Established on February 23, 1995 via Republic Act 7901, Caraga covers 21,515 km² across five provinces, two highly urbanized cities, and three component cities.

The region is defined by extraordinary natural wealth: vast tropical forests, the Agusan River basin, rich coastal ecosystems, and significant mineral deposits including nickel, gold, and coal. Butuan City holds a special place in Philippine pre-colonial history as the site of the Balanghai boat discoveries — evidence of a sophisticated seafaring civilisation dating back to at least 320 AD.

Caraga is home to indigenous peoples — the Manobo, Mamanwa, Higaonon, Banwaon, and Surigaonon — whose traditions, languages, and deep ecological knowledge continue to shape the region's identity.

Our Story

History of Caraga

From ancient seafarers to a thriving modern region — trace the remarkable journey of Caraga across 1,700 years of history.

~320 AD
Ancient History

The Balanghai Era

Ancient plank boats called Balanghai carried Austronesian settlers along the coasts of what is now Butuan. Unearthed in the 1970s in Libertad, Butuan City, these are among the oldest watercraft found in Southeast Asia. The site yielded over ten vessels alongside gold jewellery, Chinese ceramics, and burial jars — proof of a sophisticated seafaring civilisation centuries before Spanish contact.

~900–1000 AD
Pre-Colonial

The Gold Trade Period

Butuan emerged as a major gold-trading hub in Southeast Asia. Archaeological finds — golden masks, kinnari figurines, gold links, and jewellery of the finest craftsmanship — reflect a civilisation with sophisticated metallurgy and wide-ranging maritime trade networks reaching as far as Java, Borneo, and mainland China. Over 1,500 gold artefacts have been recovered from the Butuan sites.

1001 AD
Pre-Colonial

Rajah Kiling's Mission to China

The Rajahnate of Butuan's ruler, Rajah Kiling, sent an official envoy to Emperor Zhenzong of China's Song Dynasty — the earliest recorded diplomatic contact between a Philippine polity and China. The envoy offered tribute and sought trade recognition. This marked Butuan as one of the first Philippine states to engage in formal diplomacy with a major world empire.

9th–11th Century
Pre-Colonial

Rajahnate of Butuan — A Golden Kingdom

Butuan emerged as one of the Philippines' earliest and most powerful pre-colonial kingdoms — a gold-rich trading state with documented ties to China's Song Dynasty. Rajah Kiling's envoys regularly visited the Chinese imperial court. Butuan's gold artefacts — masks, jewellery, and the famous "Butuan Silver Paleograph" — testify to a cosmopolitan civilisation flourishing centuries before Magellan arrived.

March 17, 1521
Spanish Contact

Magellan's First Philippine Landing

Ferdinand Magellan's fleet made its first recorded Philippine landfall at Homonhon Island, off the coast of what is now Surigao del Norte. After weeks at sea crossing the Pacific, the crew rested, replenished water, and traded with local inhabitants. Magellan's chronicler Pigafetta recorded the warm reception of the local people. Butuan's Rajah Kolambu later guided the fleet to Cebu.

1563
Spanish Colonial

Spanish Colonisation Begins in Caraga

Spanish forces extended colonial reach into the Caraga coast. Augustinian missionaries established footholds in the region, founding mission towns along the Agusan River valley and Surigao coastlines. The Spanish referred to the entire region as "La Caraga." Indigenous peoples of the interior largely resisted pacification and maintained their culture in the highlands.

1609
Spanish Colonial

Jesuit Missions Established

Jesuit missionaries arrived in Caraga, expanding the Catholic presence into the Agusan River valley. They established reduction towns to concentrate indigenous Manobo, Mamanwa, and Higaonon populations into church-controlled settlements. Despite periodic revolts — most notably the Caraga Revolt of 1631 — the mission system gradually transformed social structures across the region.

16th–19th Century
Spanish Colonial

Three Centuries Under Spain

Spanish missionaries — primarily the Augustinian Recollects — established Catholic parishes throughout Caraga's coastal towns. Churches built during this era still stand in Butuan, Surigao, and Bislig. The colonial economy extracted timber, beeswax, abaca, and minor gold from the interior. The region's coastal towns became important stopovers on the Manila-Moluccas trade route.

Early 1900s
American Period

American Period & Modernisation

Under American administration, the region was restructured administratively. Public schools, roads, telegraph lines, and health infrastructure expanded rapidly. The Agusan River valley opened for large-scale agriculture, attracting Visayan settlers. Timber concessions and mining operations — gold, copper, and later nickel — reshaped the landscape and economy.

1941–1945
World War II

World War II — The Japanese Occupation

Japanese forces occupied Caraga from 1942, establishing bases in Butuan, Surigao, and Lianga. Filipino guerrilla units waged persistent resistance. The Battle of Surigao Strait (October 1944) — the largest naval battle in history by number of ships — was fought in adjacent waters, resulting in a decisive Allied victory. Liberation of Caraga's major towns was completed by early 1945.

1970s
Modern Era

The Balanghai Boats Unearthed

In 1976, workers at Libertad, Butuan City, accidentally uncovered the first Balanghai boat. Subsequent excavations yielded over 10 ancient vessels. Carbon dating placed the oldest at approximately 320 AD. The discovery rewrote Philippine history — proving a complex seafaring civilisation existed in Caraga over 1,700 years ago. The Butuan Balanghai Boats were declared National Cultural Treasures.

1976
Conservation

Agusan Marsh Declared a Wildlife Sanctuary

Presidential Proclamation 1038 declared the Agusan Marsh a Protected Wildlife Sanctuary. The marsh was later designated a Ramsar Wetland of International Importance in 1999, acknowledging its global significance for migratory birds, endemic wildlife (including the critically endangered Philippine freshwater crocodile), and the traditional livelihoods of Manobo communities.

1992
Tourism

Siargao Emerges on the World Stage

International surfers began arriving at Siargao Island. The wave at Cloud 9 — a powerful, barrelling right-hander over a shallow reef — was quickly recognised as one of the finest in Asia. By the mid-1990s, Siargao had begun attracting surf magazines, competitions, and a growing backpacker scene that would transform the island's economy.

February 23, 1995
Birth of Region XIII

Republic Act 7901 — Caraga Becomes Region XIII

Republic Act 7901, signed by President Fidel Ramos, formally established Caraga as a separate administrative region. Comprising four original provinces with Butuan City as the regional centre, the creation of Caraga enabled focused governance and development planning tailored to the area's unique geography and needs.

1996
Tourism

First Siargao International Surfing Cup

The inaugural Siargao International Surfing Cup was held at Cloud 9, marking the beginning of what would become one of Asia's most prestigious annual surf events. The competition drew international competitors and media attention, firmly establishing Siargao — and Caraga — on the global tourism map.

2006
Province Created

Dinagat Islands — Caraga's 5th Province

Republic Act 9355 created the Province of Dinagat Islands, separating it from Surigao del Norte. Despite a brief Supreme Court challenge, it was formally reconstituted in 2011 under Republic Act 10020. Today Dinagat stands as Caraga's newest province — known for rock formations, bioluminescent coves, Sohoton Cove, and significant nickel mineral deposits.

2012
Natural Disaster

Typhoon Pablo (Bopha) Devastates Caraga

Super Typhoon Pablo, one of the strongest typhoons ever to hit Mindanao, swept through Caraga in December 2012. The storm caused catastrophic damage to Surigao del Sur and Agusan del Sur. Thousands were displaced and infrastructure was severely damaged. The disaster significantly strengthened disaster preparedness systems in the region.

2015–Present
Global Recognition

Caraga Goes Global

The Enchanted River of Hinatuan went viral internationally. Siargao was named "Best Island in Asia" by Condé Nast Traveler in 2019 and "Best Island in the World" in 2021. Today Caraga balances sustainable development, environmental conservation, and tourism — working to protect its extraordinary natural and cultural heritage for future generations.

Culture & Festivals

Major Events & Celebrations

Caraga's vibrant festivals celebrate its indigenous roots, maritime heritage, and rich cultural tapestry.

August

Balanghai Festival

Butuan City's grandest celebration — street dancing, a fluvial parade on the Agusan River, and cultural showcases honouring the city's ancient Balanghai seafaring heritage.

Butuan City, Agusan del Norte
September

Bonok-Bonok Maradjao Kataw

Surigao City's beloved festival — a spectacular fluvial procession, street dancing, and cultural performances paying tribute to the sea and the city's maritime identity.

Surigao City, Surigao del Norte
October

Kahimunan Festival

A deeply religious fiesta in Butuan honouring the Virgen de los Remedios — colourful processions, traditional Butuanon dances, and a week-long celebration of faith and cultural pride.

Butuan City, Agusan del Norte
September

Cloud 9 Surfing Cup

One of Asia's most prestigious surfing events at the legendary Cloud 9 break in Siargao Island. International competitors ride the powerful, barrelling waves judged by world-class standards.

Siargao Island, Surigao del Norte
June

Panaghiusa sa Kinaiyahan

An environmental festival celebrating Caraga's natural heritage — mass tree planting, river clean-ups, eco-tours into the Agusan Marsh, and indigenous culture showcases.

Agusan del Sur
December

Caraga Fiesta

A region-wide celebration bringing all five provinces together — trade fairs, cultural performances, indigenous craft markets, and food festivals showcasing the best of Caraga.

Region-wide, Caraga
